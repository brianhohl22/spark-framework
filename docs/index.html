👨‍👩‍👧‍👦 Families & Community Members

Parents and community members are often the first to identify unmet needs or exciting possibilities.

You might be thinking: "My child would thrive in an arts-focused school, but nothing like that exists in SUSD" or "Other districts offer XYZ — why don't we?"
Real-world example: A parent group notices students leaving for a nearby charter with a STEM focus. They organize feedback showing demand for similar programming within SUSD. Their input enters as a community interest submission.

You don't need a fully developed proposal. A clear description of the need and who it would serve is a great start.

🏫 Educators & School Staff

Teachers and principals see what works (and what's missing) every day. Site-level innovation is a powerful entry point.

You might be thinking: "My elective pilot has strong student outcomes — could this become a signature program?" or "Our school needs a pathway that connects to local employers."
Real-world example: A teacher proposes an entrepreneurship elective based on strong student engagement. It starts as a site-level pilot and, with evidence of impact, enters the SPARK pipeline for possible expansion.

Your classroom insights and student data are invaluable. Include any evidence of impact or student interest you have.

💡 Innovation Grants & Partner-Driven Opportunities

External funding and partnerships can accelerate innovation — but sustainability matters as much as the initial opportunity.

You might be thinking: "We have a grant opportunity that could fund a new STEM lab — how do we evaluate if it fits SUSD's strategy?"
Real-world example: A university partner offers funding for a dual-enrollment program. SPARK evaluates not just the grant terms but the long-term viability after funding ends, ensuring it aligns with district priorities.

Funding is an important factor but not the only criterion. Include the sustainability plan and alignment to district goals.

🛠️ CTE Pathways & Career Readiness

Career and Technical Education pathways connect students to workforce opportunities and align with Arizona's Profile of a Graduate.

You might be thinking: "Local employers are asking for specific skills. Could we build a pathway that gives students real credentials?"
Real-world example: The CTE team identifies growing demand for healthcare careers. They propose a health sciences pathway with hospital partnerships and industry certifications, entering through the structured intake process.

⭐ SPARK Team & Subgroups

SPARK members identify opportunities through committee work, concept studies, and portfolio analysis. Subgroups (e.g., Gifted/Advanced, Arts) develop concepts collaboratively.

You might be thinking: "Our concept study shows strong benchmarks for a gifted hub — we're ready to formalize this into a proposal."
Real-world example: The SPARK landscape review reveals an unmet demand segment. A concept study subgroup is formed, benchmarks are gathered, and when the concept matures, it enters the formal intake pipeline with evidence and a recommended model.

🔍 Landscape Scan & Proactive Discovery

SPARK doesn't just wait for ideas to arrive — it actively scans for trends, gaps, and opportunities through periodic landscape assessments.

What this looks like: Quarterly review of enrollment trends, nearby district/charter/private program launches, national education trends, workforce signals, and mobility/choice data.
Real-world example: Enrollment data shows students leaving a feeder pattern for a nearby charter. SPARK investigates whether a programmatic response (not necessarily a new program) could address the underlying need.

🎓 Student Voice

Students are the ultimate beneficiaries of specialty programs. Their perspectives on what engages, challenges, and inspires them are essential input.

You might be thinking: "I wish my school had a class where I could build real things" or "I want to study something my school doesn't offer."
How it works: Student input comes through surveys, student government, and direct feedback channels. SPARK considers student interest data as a key demand signal when evaluating new concepts.

Structured Intake

All ideas enter through a consistent Intake Form — a 15-minute structured questionnaire aligned to SUSD's Quality Indicators. This ensures every concept is captured with enough detail for fair review.

Preliminary Screen

SPARK leadership performs a quick check: Is this a duplicate? Is there an obvious blocker? Does it meet a minimum viability threshold? Ideas that pass move to Evaluate. Others are parked in the registry (tracked, not lost) or redirected to an existing program.

Periodic Landscape Scan

Quarterly, SPARK reviews: open enrollment trends, nearby district/charter/private program launches, national education trends, and workforce signals. Findings feed directly into the Idea Registry.

Framework › Evaluate

Evaluate — How SPARK Assesses

SPARK uses consistent, QI-aligned evaluation for both new concepts entering the pipeline and existing programs seeking enhancement. The same quality standards apply across the board.

New Concept Review

New ideas are reviewed against SUSD's 6 Quality Indicator categories to assess alignment, demand, instructional quality, feasibility, financial sustainability, and community fit.

Quality Indicator Categories:

  • Mission & Strategic Alignment
  • Student Demand & Access
  • Instructional Quality
  • Operational Feasibility
  • Financial Sustainability
  • Community & Stakeholder Alignment

Specific scoring methodology and thresholds are under development with the SPARK team.

Existing Program Enhancement

Existing programs have a proven thesis — approved by the Board and valued by the community. SPARK's role is to identify opportunities to enhance and uplift these programs, using the QI framework as a lens for continuous improvement.

The Program Review sub-team works with program leads to surface strengths, identify gaps, and recommend targeted investments that help established programs reach their full potential.

Current focus: MSA (Math/Science Academy) and STEM programs are under collaborative review.

Deep Review

Concepts that advance from initial review are assigned to a SPARK Design Team (new concepts) or Review Team (existing programs). These sub-teams conduct comprehensive evaluation using the full Quality Indicator framework, evidence packs, and stakeholder input.

Implementation & Monitoring

Approved programs receive tiered implementation plans with measurable goals, designated program leads, and capacity-building support (staffing, funding, professional learning). SPARK monitors progress and reports annually to district leadership.

Communication & Stakeholder Engagement

Every decision comes with a clear "why." SPARK supports transparent communication to families and community — sharing value, purpose, and progress of specialty programs across the district.

How Ideas Enter the SPARK Pipeline

Program ideas reach SPARK through multiple channels — each with a clear pathway and appropriate level of review. The table below shows the primary entry channels and how each is handled.

ChannelExample TriggerWho InitiatesTypical PathwayKey Considerations
Board-Directed Study Board asks district to study feasibility of a program model Governing Board (via Superintendent) Concept Study → Intake & Full Review Study may be required, but implementation is contingent on evidence & constraints
Leadership-Initiated Superintendent or cabinet commissions exploration District leadership Concept Study or Intake & Triage (depending on scale) Light triage/charter can clarify scope before deep work
Community Interest Parent group requests specialized programming Families / community groups Intake Form → Screen → Concept Study or Full Review Transparent criteria; equity/access design; check for duplicates via portfolio
School / Site Proposal Teachers propose a signature program for their school Site leader / school team Intake Form → Triage → Full Review Clarify site-specific vs. scalable; staffing/facilities impacts
Grant / Partner Opportunity External partner offers funding or program Grants office / partner Fast Triage → Full Review with conditions Sustainability beyond grant period; not funding-driven alone
Informal Idea Staff or community member shares idea via email Anyone Light Submission → Idea Log → Screen SPARK helps develop and route; no sponsor required initially
SPARK Landscape Scan Portfolio gap or unmet demand identified SPARK committee / Review team Concept Study / Feasibility Scan Proactive discovery; contributes to SPARK's intake capability

When Does a Concept Study Become a Formal Proposal?

A concept recommended to proceed converts to a formal intake by producing: a named sponsor with authority, a clarified model choice, a preliminary resource plan, a draft evaluation plan (success metrics & benchmarks), documented equity/access design, and a communications plan.

These scenarios illustrate how different types of program ideas enter and move through the SPARK pipeline.

Assistant Principal emails a program idea

SPARK's portfolio analysis shows an underserved grade band for dual language. A sponsor is recruited to explore options using benchmarks and mobility data.

University partner offers grant-funded STEM lab

A time-limited grant opportunity arrives. SPARK fast-triages for alignment and sustainability — funding is important but not the only criterion. Requires a "post-grant" sustainability plan.

Enrollment shifts and available space trigger exploration. SPARK routes to concept study with facilities data, includes transportation and equity analysis from day one.

A teacher's entrepreneurship elective shows strong engagement. SPARK encourages the site-level pilot, logs it, and schedules a review checkpoint for possible scale-up.

Key Principle

Every scenario follows the same framework: capture the idea with enough structure, evaluate it against consistent criteria, and communicate the outcome transparently. The pathway adapts to the source and scale — but the standards remain consistent.
